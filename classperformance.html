25 Performance of Classifiers

How do we measure the performance of a classifier? In a classification setting we still have response observations \(y_i\) and predicted values \(\hat{y}_i\); we still want a way to compare \(y_i\) and \(\hat{y}_i\). We could try to use a quadratic pointwise error \((y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2\), and then combined them into a Mean Squared Error (MSE) measure like we did in regression.

\[ \mathrm{MSE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2 \]

unfortunately, \(\text{MSE}\) will no longer work; as such, we need to develop a new error measure.

25.1 Error for Binary Response

What if our \(y\) values are binary; that is, \(y_i \in \{0, 1\}\). Then, we could try the following pointwise error measure:

\[ \left| y_i - \hat{y}_i \right| = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } y_i = \hat{y}_i \\ 1 & \text{if } y_i \neq \hat{y}_i \\ \end{cases} \]

Now, suppose we still had binary data but instead we encoded our data so that \(y_i \in \{-1, 1\}\). What kind of pointwise error measure should we use? We could use the following:

\[ (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if agreement}\\ 4 & \text{if disagreement} \\ \end{cases} \]

Alternatively, we could use

\[ y_i \times \hat{y}_i = \begin{cases} -1 & \text{if agreement} \\ 1 & \text{if agreement} \\ \end{cases} \]

The point is: there are many different choices for pointwise error measures! In fact, there isnâ€™t really a single agreed-upon measure in this case.

25.2 Categorical Response

Now suppose, for example, \(y_i \in \{a, b, c, d\}\). That is, \(y_i\) is still categorical but no longer binary. For illustrative purposes, consider the following data:

\[ \begin{array}{c|ccccc} y & a & b & c & d & b \\ \hline \hat{y} & a & a & c & d & c \\ \hline & \checkmark & \times & \checkmark & \checkmark & \times \\ \end{array} \]

(here the symbol \(\checkmark\) denotes agreement between the observed values and the predicted values). In this case, it makes sense to use the binary error as our pointwise error measure, defined to be

\[ [\![ \hat{y}_i \neq y_i ]\!] \to \begin{cases} 1 \ (\text{or TRUE}) & \text{if disagree} \\ 0 \ (\text{or FALSE}) & \text{if agree} \\ \end{cases} \]

Averaging these binary errors over our observations yields the misclassification error rate:

\[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} [\![ \hat{y}_i \neq y_i ]\!] \]

25.3 Confusion Matrices

In this categorical-but-not-binary case, it is customary to construct a confusion matrix.

Any entry in this matrix contains information about how our predicted classifications compare with the observed classifications. For example, the number in the \((1, 2)\) entry represents the number of observations that were classified as being of class \(a\) when in fact they were actually in class \(b\). As such, the off-diagonal entries represent entries that were misclassified. (By convention, diagonal entries are set to be the number of observations in each class). Hence, we can recover our misclassification error from the confusion matrix using the formula

\[ 1 - \frac{\sum (\text{all cells in diagonal}) }{n} = \text{Misclassification Error} \]

In this way, the â€œidealâ€ confusion matrix is one that is completely diagonal, with all off-diagonal entries equal to \(0\). The higher the off-diagonal entries, the worse our model is performing.

25.4 Binary Response: Example

Consider an approval application process. That is, we look at all the steps a customer at a certain bank would need to take in order to get approval for something: for example

  • Credit Card
  • Car Loan
  • Mortgage
  • Savings/Checking Account
  • Moneymarket Account
  • Insurance policy

For each of these applications, there will be some series of questions a customer needs to answer: for example

  • Do you have a bank account?
  • Are you a US citizen?
  • Are you over the age of 18?
  • What is your income?

That is, we want a model \(f\) that takes the answers to these questions \(\mathbf{x_i}\), and spits out either accept or reject. Pictorially:

\[ \mathbf{x_i} \to \boxed{\hat{f}} \to \hat{y}_i = \begin{cases} \text{accept} \\ \text{reject} \\ \end{cases} \]

25.4.1 Types of Errors: Application for Savings Account

The first kind of error is a false rejection. That is, suppose a customer fills out an application and, despite the fact that they are theoretically eligible to open a savings account, is rejected. The second kind of error is a false acceptance; that is, a customer who is (theoretical) a bad customer, still gets approved to open an account.

Which type of error is worse from the bankâ€™s point of view? A false rejection! This is because this â€œgoodâ€ customer would likely be able to go across the street and open up an account at the rival bank; that is, the initial bank has lost a potential customer!

25.4.2 Another Example: Loans

Now, suppose that the application in question is for a mortgage. The two types of errors still stand: false rejection and false acceptance. In this case, which error is more costly (from the bankâ€™s point of view)? False acceptance; if the bank grants a loan to someone who wonâ€™t be able to pay it back in the long run, the bank will lose money (likely more money than they would have gained through interest from somebody who could have paid their loan)! Hence, we might assign the following penalties:

  • False Rejection: The bank loses money that they could have gathered through interest from the customer.

  • False Acceptance: The bank loses money because the customer will ultimately default and not pay back the loan.

25.4.3 The Ideal Situation

In an ideal situation, we would simultaneously minimize the false acceptance and false rejection rates. However, as with everything else in Machine Learning, there is a tradeoff; in general, minimizing false acceptance rates increases false rejection rates, and vice-versa.
